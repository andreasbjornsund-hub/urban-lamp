Why you need a certified enclosure

Workers in oil refineries, chemical plants, offshore platforms, and pharmaceutical facilities need reliable communication tools. Standard smartphones — including iPhones — are not designed for environments where explosive gases, vapors, or dust particles could ignite from a single spark. That is where ATEX and IECEx certified enclosures come in.

These certifications are not marketing labels. ATEX is a European Union directive covering equipment used in potentially explosive atmospheres, while IECEx is the international certification system recognized across more than 30 countries. Zone 1 classification specifically covers areas where explosive atmospheres are likely to occur during normal operations — not just occasionally.

If your team relies on iPhones for communication, data logging, inspections, or remote collaboration in these environments, you need an enclosure that meets Zone 1 requirements without turning the phone into an unusable brick. The enclosure must manage thermal output, prevent spark generation, and contain any potential ignition source — while still letting workers actually use the device comfortably.

Understanding ATEX/IECEx Zone 1 Certification

What Zone 1 means in practice

Hazardous areas are classified into zones based on the frequency and duration of explosive atmosphere presence. Zone 0 means an explosive atmosphere is present continuously. Zone 1 means it is likely to occur during normal operations. Zone 2 means it is unlikely but possible. Most active work areas in oil and gas, petrochemical, and mining operations fall under Zone 1 classification.

An enclosure certified for Zone 1 must prevent the iPhone's battery, circuitry, screen, and connectors from becoming ignition sources. This involves controlling surface temperatures, eliminating electrostatic discharge risks, and ensuring that any internal fault cannot propagate to the external environment.

EX certified enclosures vs rugged cases

Many buyers confuse industrial rugged cases with genuine EX certified enclosures. Rugged cases protect your phone from drops, water, and dust. They do absolutely nothing to prevent explosion risks. A rugged case has no spark prevention, no thermal management, and no certification from a notified body.

An EX certified enclosure goes through rigorous third-party testing. The manufacturer must demonstrate compliance with specific protection concepts such as Ex ia (intrinsic safety), Ex ib, or Ex d (flameproof enclosure). Each protection concept has strict requirements for energy limitation, enclosure integrity, and component selection.

Key certification markings to look for

When evaluating enclosures, check for these specific markings: the ATEX marking (hexagonal Ex symbol followed by group, category, and temperature class), the IECEx certificate number, and the specific protection concept designation. A legitimate Zone 1 product will carry markings like "Ex ib IIC T4 Gb" or similar, indicating the gas group, temperature class, and equipment protection level.

If a product only references IP ratings such as IP68 or MIL-STD drop specifications without ATEX/IECEx certificate numbers, it is not certified for hazardous areas. Do not bring it into a Zone 1 environment.

How to evaluate and select the right enclosure

1

Confirm your zone classification

Work with your facility's safety engineer or hazardous area classification specialist to confirm the exact zone, gas group, and temperature class requirements for your work areas. An enclosure rated for Zone 2 will not legally or safely work in Zone 1.

2

Verify certification authenticity

Request the actual ATEX or IECEx certificate from the manufacturer. Cross-reference the certificate number with the IECEx online certificate system at iecex.iec.ch or the relevant EU notified body database. Counterfeit or expired certifications exist in this market.

3

Assess iPhone model compatibility

Not all enclosures support every iPhone generation. Verify that the enclosure is designed and certified for your specific model. Certification is model-specific because different iPhone versions have different battery capacities, charging characteristics, and thermal profiles.

4

Evaluate ergonomics and usability

This is where most enclosures fail. Many certified solutions are bulky, heavy, and nearly impossible to operate with gloved hands. Look for enclosures that maintain full touchscreen functionality, camera access, and reasonable weight. Workers who find the device uncomfortable will find workarounds — and workarounds in hazardous areas are dangerous. Xshielder's approach focuses specifically on preserving the iPhone's native ergonomics while meeting Zone 1 requirements.

5

Consider total cost of ownership

The purchase price is just the beginning. Factor in replacement part availability, warranty terms, recertification requirements after repairs, and accessory compatibility. A cheaper enclosure that requires frequent gasket replacements or full unit replacement after minor damage will cost more over its operational life.

Pro tips

  • Choose a manufacturer that provides clear documentation on which iPhone models are currently supported. Certification is not transferable between phone models.
  • Always register your enclosure with the manufacturer for safety notices, firmware updates, and recertification reminders.
  • Train your workforce on proper enclosure handling. Inspect gaskets and seals before every shift.
  • Store spare gaskets and screen protectors on site. Waiting weeks for replacement parts means operating without protection or losing functionality.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Don't confuse rugged with certified. Products marketed as rugged phones lack Zone 1 EX certification. Using them in classified areas violates safety regulations.
  • Don't modify your enclosure. Drilling holes for lanyards, applying adhesive mounts, or altering the charging port cover invalidates the certification immediately.
  • Don't use damaged enclosures. Cracked windows, deformed housings, or hardened gaskets compromise the protection concept.
  • Don't use Zone 2 equipment in Zone 1. The protection requirements are fundamentally different, and Zone 2 equipment in Zone 1 is a regulatory violation.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between ATEX and IECEx certification?

ATEX is mandatory for equipment sold and used within the European Economic Area. IECEx is an international system recognized globally. Many manufacturers obtain both to cover worldwide markets. The technical requirements are closely aligned but administered by different bodies.

Can I use any iPhone model with a Zone 1 enclosure?

No. Each enclosure is certified for specific iPhone models. The certification accounts for the phone's electrical characteristics, battery capacity, and thermal behavior. Always confirm model compatibility before purchasing.

How long does a certified enclosure last?

Most enclosures have a service life of 3 to 5 years depending on environmental conditions and usage intensity. Gaskets and seals typically require replacement annually. Follow the manufacturer's maintenance schedule.

Does Xshielder support the latest iPhone models?

Yes. Xshielder actively maintains certifications for current-generation Apple iPhones, including the iPhone 17 and iPhone 17 Pro Max, ensuring that users can upgrade their phones without losing hazardous area access.

Are there Zone 1 enclosures for Android phones?

Some manufacturers offer Android enclosures, but the iPhone ecosystem is preferred in many industrial settings due to consistent hardware specifications across units, which simplifies the certification process.

Can I charge my iPhone inside the enclosure in a Zone 1 area?

Charging in a Zone 1 area is generally not permitted unless the charging system itself is certified for that zone. Remove the device to a safe area for charging.

How do I verify that a certificate is still valid?

Visit the IECEx online certificate database at iecex.iec.ch and enter the certificate number. For ATEX, contact the issuing notified body directly.
